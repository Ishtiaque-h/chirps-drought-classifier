# Manuscript Results and Discussion Draft

Working title: **A Predictability Audit of Lag-Based Machine Learning for Monthly Drought Forecasting**

This draft is tied to `results/paper/`, generated by
`scripts/build_paper_evidence_pack.py`. Treat the tables and figures in that
folder as the source of truth for numerical claims.

## Core Claim

Lag-based machine-learning models detect drought-relevant hydroclimate
structure, but they rarely convert that structure into robust calibrated
monthly probability skill over climatology. This conclusion holds after
enforcing leakage-safe targets, monthly-level uncertainty, validation-only
calibration, source-cited regional masks, rolling temporal holdouts,
independent PRISM validation, and seasonal/long-lead target checks.

This framing is stronger than claiming a positive forecast model. The paper is
best positioned as a **predictability and evaluation audit** for drought ML:
it shows how much skill remains after removing common sources of inflated
performance.

## Suggested Results Section

### 1. Calibrated Central Valley Forecast Skill Is Statistically Indistinguishable From Climatology

The primary Central Valley forecast target is monthly SPI-1 dry fraction at
lead 1, evaluated over 63 independent test months. The best calibrated
checkpoint is XGB-Spatial with validation-selected isotonic calibration. It
achieves a small positive Brier Skill Score over monthly climatology
(`BSS = +0.005`), but the 95% bootstrap interval crosses zero
(`[-0.062, +0.073]`; `table02_headline_results.csv`; Fig. 1).

This result should be read as a practical tie with climatology. The model has
ranking signal, but calibrated dry-probability skill is not robust enough for
an operational positive-skill claim. The result also illustrates why pixel-wise
accuracy is insufficient: monthly BSS is the relevant inferential unit because
grid cells within a month are spatially autocorrelated.

Suggested manuscript wording:

> The best Central Valley checkpoint approached but did not significantly
> exceed monthly climatology. XGB-Spatial with validation-selected isotonic
> calibration produced `BSS = +0.005` over 63 test months, with a 95% bootstrap
> interval spanning zero. We therefore interpret the result as evidence of weak
> ranking information but no robust calibrated probability skill.

Primary evidence: `table02_headline_results.csv`, `fig01_headline_bss_forest.png`.

### 2. Added Land-Surface, Atmospheric, Deep-Learning, and Operational Inputs Do Not Resolve the Skill Gap

Feature-extension experiments do not overturn the climatology-tie result.
Gridded ERA5-Land temperature/VPD with XGB-Spatial remains statistically
indistinguishable from climatology (`BSS = -0.047`, CI `[-0.131, +0.023]`).
Regional temperature/VPD, soil moisture, and MJO/IVT extensions are robustly
negative in the current checkpoint (`BSS = -0.092`, `-0.158`, and `-0.333`,
respectively; `table02_headline_results.csv`).

The EDL uncertainty experiment also underperforms climatology
(`BSS = -0.116`, CI `[-0.207, -0.059]`), so it is useful as a negative
uncertainty-quantification checkpoint, not as the main novelty claim.
Forecast-informed NMME precipitation products add a more relevant operational
comparison. The SPI-1 lead-1 NMME anomaly benchmark is tied with climatology
(`BSS = +0.002`, CI `[-0.438, +0.239]`). A seasonal NMME SPI-3 lead-3 anomaly
benchmark improves the point estimate (`BSS = +0.086`) and has useful rank
signal, but its interval still crosses zero (`[-0.225, +0.261]`). The matched
SPI-6 lead-6 anomaly benchmark is below climatology (`BSS = -0.344`, CI
`[-1.130, +0.113]`). Official CPC below-normal probability benchmarks add a
direct probabilistic comparator: selected SPI-1 lead-1 reaches `BSS = +0.131`
over 52 valid test months, but the interval still crosses zero
(`[-0.304, +0.338]`), and raw SPI-3/SPI-6 probability point estimates remain
small (`+0.007` and `+0.035`). This matters because real external forecast
signals are detectable, but they still do not produce robust probability skill
under the current monthly dry-fraction protocol.

Suggested manuscript wording:

> None of the added predictor, architecture, or operational-forecast paths
> produced robust positive probability skill. The strongest operational point
> estimates come from CPC NMME probability and anomaly products, but all
> confidence intervals still cross zero. The evidence therefore points less to
> model capacity and more to the information content and calibration limits of
> monthly-to-seasonal drought probability prediction.

Primary evidence: `table02_headline_results.csv`, `fig01_headline_bss_forest.png`.

### 3. The Weak-Skill Conclusion Is Not an Artifact of the 2021-2026 Test Window

The canonical test period includes both severe drought and a wet reversal, so
test-window representativeness is a legitimate concern. The rolling temporal
audit addresses this by retraining tabular XGBoost across five chronological
Central Valley holdouts. Against train-period calendar-month climatology,
all five BSS point estimates are non-positive:

- 2005-2008: `BSS = -0.089`
- 2009-2012: `BSS = -0.004`
- 2013-2016: `BSS = -0.077`
- 2017-2020: `BSS = -0.010`
- 2021-2026: `BSS = -0.025`

All intervals cross zero (`table04_temporal_robustness.csv`; Fig. 4). The
canonical 2021-2026 block is therefore not the only weak evaluation window.
Within that test period, the main failure mode remains underprediction during
the 2021-2022 drought, followed by closer-to-climatology behavior during the
2023 wet reversal and the later test months.

Suggested manuscript wording:

> Rolling holdout results indicate that weak skill is not explained solely by
> the unusual 2021-2026 test period. Across five chronological Central Valley
> holdouts, no split produced a positive BSS point estimate against
> train-period monthly climatology.

Primary evidence: `table04_temporal_robustness.csv`, `fig04_temporal_holdout_bss.png`.

### 4. Multi-Region Evaluation Supports Generalization, But Not Robust Positive Skill

The source-cited regional path evaluates Central Valley, Southern Great Plains,
Murray-Darling, Mediterranean Spain, and Horn of Africa checkpoints. Regional
geometry matters: retained valid CHIRPS cells range from 27.9% for the Central
Valley basin mask to 87.3% for the Horn of Africa country-intersection mask
(`table03_mask_methods.csv`; Fig. 5). This justifies treating rectangular
bounding boxes as sensitivities rather than final scientific regions.

Across the selected multi-region SPI-1 lead-1 checkpoints, positive point
estimates remain uncertain. Mediterranean Spain rectangular tabular XGBoost
has `BSS = +0.044`, but the confidence interval crosses zero
(`[-0.151, +0.241]`). The Southern Great Plains basin-mask checkpoint is also
positive but uncertain (`BSS = +0.010`, CI `[-0.098, +0.150]`). Murray-Darling
basin-masked tabular XGBoost is robustly negative (`BSS = -0.639`, CI
`[-1.308, -0.278]`). Horn of Africa country-masked XGB-Spatial remains tied
with climatology (`BSS = -0.031`, CI `[-0.302, +0.320]`).

Suggested manuscript wording:

> Expanding beyond California did not reveal a stable positive-skill regime for
> the current lag-based monthly target. Some regions produced positive point
> estimates, but these were geometry-sensitive and statistically uncertain,
> while other regions were robustly negative.

Primary evidence: `table02_headline_results.csv`, `table03_mask_methods.csv`,
`fig02_multiregion_bss_forest.png`, `fig05_mask_retention.png`.

### 5. Seasonal and Longer-Accumulation Targets Do Not Provide a Broad Timescale Fix

Regionalization suggested a plausible target-timescale mismatch: SPI-12 zones
show strong teleconnection structure, but monthly SPI-1 lead-1 forecast skill
does not reliably improve. Seasonal SPI-3/SPI-6 long-lead tests were therefore
run as a direct check.

The seasonal results do not support a broad timescale fix. The only robust
positive row is Mediterranean Spain basin-masked SPI-6 lead-6 with
Niño3.4-only climate features (`BSS = +0.078`, CI `[+0.004, +0.162]`).
However, the signal audit classifies this as a calibration-shift exception:
the calibrated dry-fraction correlation with observed dry fraction is only
`r = 0.041`, and the calibrated variance ratio is `0.104`
(`table05_seasonal_signal_audit.csv`; Fig. 3). In contrast, Southern Great
Plains SPI-6 lead-6 is robustly negative, Murray-Darling seasonal runs are
robustly negative, and Horn of Africa country-mask seasonal runs remain below
climatology with wide uncertainty.

Suggested manuscript wording:

> Longer accumulation targets expose useful diagnostics but do not yet produce
> broad event-tracking forecast skill. The one robust-positive seasonal row is
> better interpreted as calibration shrinkage than as successful timing of dry
> events.

Primary evidence: `table05_seasonal_signal_audit.csv`,
`fig03_seasonal_bss_vs_tracking.png`.

### 6. Regionalization Reveals Mechanism Signal That Does Not Reliably Convert Into Forecast Skill

SPI-12 regionalization provides mechanism evidence. Horn of Africa shows a
strong Niño3.4 lag-6 teleconnection in zone 0 (`r = +0.542`), Murray-Darling
has a strong Niño3.4 lag-1 relationship in zone 0 (`r = -0.460`), and Southern
Great Plains has a strong Niño3.4 lag-6 relationship in zone 3 (`r = +0.501`;
`table06_regionalization_mechanism.csv`). These signals are scientifically
meaningful because they show that climate-memory structure exists in the
observed drought fields.

But zone-level forecast diagnostics show weak conversion into skill. The
maximum zone-level BSS remains negative in Central Valley, Mediterranean
Spain, and Murray-Darling, and only isolated zones in Horn of Africa and
Southern Great Plains are positive. This supports the paper's central
distinction: physical or statistical signal can be present without yielding
robust calibrated forecast probability skill at the chosen target and lead.

Suggested manuscript wording:

> SPI-12 regionalization shows interpretable teleconnection structure, but this
> mechanism evidence does not automatically imply forecast utility. We
> therefore use regionalization to explain possible drought-memory regimes,
> not as proof that the current forecast model is skillful.

Primary evidence: `table06_regionalization_mechanism.csv`.

## Suggested Discussion Section

### Main Interpretation

The completed experiments converge on a consistent explanation: lagged
precipitation, SPI history, climate indices, and regional context contain some
drought-relevant information, but the signal is too weak, unstable, or
calibration-sensitive to produce robust monthly dry-fraction BSS over
climatology. This is especially important for drought ML because conventional
pixel-level accuracy can look useful even when monthly probability skill is
absent.

The paper should emphasize three methodological contributions:

1. **Leakage-safe target design.** Forecast features are restricted to time
   `t` or earlier, while the canonical target is SPI-1 at `t+1`.
2. **Monthly-level inference.** Skill uncertainty is evaluated over independent
   months rather than spatially autocorrelated pixels.
3. **Generalization controls.** Results are checked under validation-only
   calibration, independent PRISM precipitation, source-cited regional masks,
   rolling temporal holdouts, and seasonal target variants.

### Why Positive Point Estimates Should Not Be Overclaimed

Several experiments produce positive point estimates, but most confidence
intervals cross zero. The only robust-positive row in the consolidated master
evidence table is the Mediterranean Spain SPI-6 lead-6 seasonal case, and its
event-tracking diagnostics are weak. This is precisely why the paper should
separate **calibrated BSS** from **event tracking**. A lower Brier Score can
arise from better calibration or shrinkage toward a favorable test-period mean
without actually tracking drought fluctuations.

### Why This Is Still Scientifically Valuable

The negative result is not a failure of the project. It is the core scientific
finding. Many drought ML studies report classification accuracy or pixel-level
metrics without fully controlling leakage, spatial pseudo-replication,
climatological baselines, and calibration. This project shows that once those
controls are imposed, apparent skill largely collapses to climatology. That is
a publishable contribution because it gives the field a more rigorous
benchmark for what operational drought ML must beat.

### Remaining Limitations

1. **Forecast-informed predictors are still limited.** The current NMME path now
   includes lead-1, SPI-3 lead-3, and SPI-6 lead-6 precipitation-anomaly and
   official probability benchmarks, but it is still not a full ensemble-member
   hindcast or SubX benchmark. CPC probability coverage is also partial for
   Central Valley dry-season targets.
2. **Horn of Africa geometry is still a caveat.** The country-intersection mask
   is useful for a first-pass land-area check but is not a livelihood-zone,
   basin, or agroecological-region mask.
3. **PRISM validation is U.S.-only.** Independent precipitation-product
   validation is strongest for Central Valley; analogous validation in the
   international regions would require region-appropriate reference products.
4. **The EDL result is not a mature uncertainty contribution.** It underperforms
   climatology and should be presented as a negative UQ checkpoint unless
   further developed.
5. **Mechanism evidence is not causal attribution.** SHAP and regionalization
   identify model associations and drought-zone structure, but they do not
   prove causal drivers.

### Best Next Experiment If the Paper Needs One More Positive-Skill Attempt

Do not add another ML-only architecture now. The most defensible remaining
experiment, if the paper needs one more, is a richer forecast-informed
benchmark:

- Full NMME ensemble-member/hindcast precipitation inputs for SPI-3/SPI-6
  seasonal targets, building on the current NMME anomaly and probability
  benchmarks; or
- SubX/GEFS/ECMWF-style subseasonal precipitation/circulation inputs if the
  paper reframes toward weeks 3-4; or
- a zone-level seasonal target that directly uses the SPI-12 regionalization
  zones as forecast units.

The reason is simple: the current evidence points to information-content
limits, not a lack of model complexity.
